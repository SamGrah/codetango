# Author's Voice Guide

Read this before writing or reviewing any blog post. This defines the authorial voice for CodeTango and takes precedence over generic tone guidance elsewhere.

## Core Principle: Personal Experience, Not Prescription

Sam writes from personal experience, not from authority. The blog documents what he's built, tried, and learned — not what everyone should do. The field is moving fast, and definitive claims age poorly.

## Do

- **Frame as personal experience**: "I landed on X because Y" / "What worked for me was..." / "In my setup..."
- **Show your reasoning**: Explain *why* you chose something, not just *what*. The reasoning is the value.
- **Acknowledge uncertainty**: "I'm not sure this scales beyond..." / "This might not hold once..."
- **Invite the reader in**: Write as if you're showing a colleague your workbench, not lecturing from a podium.
- **Be specific about your context**: What made this the right choice *for this project*? Readers can extrapolate.
- **Use precise language**: Exact terms, real numbers, concrete examples — vagueness is worse than hedging.

## Don't

- **Make universal declarations**: Avoid "Agentic development is X" / "Agents should have Y" / "The right way to..."
- **Prescribe for the reader**: Avoid "You should..." / "You need to..." / "Your agents must..."
- **Claim expertise you haven't earned**: This is a blog about learning in public, not a textbook.
- **Hedge so much you say nothing**: There's a difference between honest uncertainty and waffling. Take a stance on your own experience — just don't generalize it.
- **Use filler authority phrases**: "It's well known that..." / "Best practice dictates..." / "Industry standard is..."

## The Balance

Opinionated about *your own choices*, humble about *universal truths*. You can say "I chose X over Y because Z" with full conviction. You shouldn't say "X is better than Y" as though it's settled science.

Good: "I found that committing the issue tracker to the repo removed an entire class of state-sync problems — at least for a solo project."

Bad: "You should always commit your issue tracker to the repo. External issue trackers introduce unnecessary state synchronization overhead."

## Audience Assumption

Write for someone who is sharp, curious, and probably building their own version of this. They don't need to be convinced that agentic development matters — they're already here. They want to see *how you did it* and *what you learned*, so they can steal the good parts.
